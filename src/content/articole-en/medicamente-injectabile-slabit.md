You've probably heard the names at least once in recent months: Ozempic, Mounjaro, Wegovy. They've become a topic of discussion on TV, on social media, and in everyday conversations, and Google searches have exploded. Because so many people are looking for information about them, we've prepared a **strictly informative and neutral** overview — what they are, why they're being talked about so much, and why, regardless of what you've heard, the decision regarding them belongs exclusively to a doctor.

> **Important from the start:** this article **does not recommend and does not discourage** the use of these medications and **does not evaluate** how well they work. They are medications issued **only on prescription**. The only person who can say whether they are suitable (or not) for someone is **a doctor**. Our purpose is only to explain, at a general level, what they are.

## What they are, in brief

These are medications administered by injection that were initially developed in a medical context — primarily for managing type 2 diabetes. At a general level, they act on mechanisms related to appetite and blood glucose regulation, which is why some of them have subsequently received approvals for weight management as well, under conditions established by regulatory authorities and doctors.

The names people hear — Ozempic, Mounjaro, Wegovy, and others — are **brand names**. They are not off-the-shelf products, not supplements, and not "natural." They are serious medications, prescribed and used **only under medical supervision**, exactly like any other prescription treatment.

## Why they're being talked about so much

In large part, public attention is a **phenomenon that started overseas**, in the US, and especially in Hollywood, from where it spread around the world. The discussion exploded when several well-known personalities **spoke openly** about having used such medications — among them:

- **Oprah Winfrey**, who publicly declared that she uses a weight loss medication "as a tool";
- **Elon Musk**, who himself mentioned on social media fasting and such a medication;
- **Whoopi Goldberg**, who spoke about having used such a treatment on a talk show.

We mention them strictly as **examples of the public discussion** — we do not evaluate and do not confirm results, and what worked (or didn't) for a public figure says nothing about what is appropriate for someone else. A celebrity has access to constant medical monitoring; a viral message on the internet doesn't come with a doctor attached. Every case is different, and the decision remains, again, a **medical and individual** one.

## Why they're not the "ready-made solution" the internet implies

The way this subject circulates on social media creates a false impression: that it's some kind of cost-free shortcut. There are, however, a few things worth keeping in mind, without going into medical detail.

First, like any prescription medication, they can have **side effects** — and that's precisely why they're issued only by prescription and used under a doctor's supervision, who weighs the benefits and risks for each individual person. They're not sweets.

Second, one aspect frequently discussed publicly by specialists is that **they do not replace lifestyle change**. They are presented, including by doctors, as a tool used alongside diet and movement, not in place of them. The underlying mechanism of weight loss remains a [calorie deficit](/dieta/deficit-caloric/) — the medication, when prescribed, is just one piece of the puzzle, not the whole picture.

## Why we don't provide dosage or administration details

Intentionally, this article does **not** include doses, administration schedules, or how to obtain these medications. The reason is simple: these are **medical** decisions, individualised, based on the evaluation of a specialist with knowledge of each person's history. What is appropriate for one person may be inappropriate or even risky for another. Any such detail taken from outside a doctor's office — from a forum, a clip, a friend — can be dangerous.

## What remains true, regardless of the subject

One thing doesn't change, no matter how many new medication names appear: **there's no solution that eliminates the need for healthy habits**. Balanced nutrition, movement, sleep, and patience remain the foundation for everyone, with or without treatment. ## Frequently asked questions

### Can I take these medications to lose weight if I don't have diabetes?
Only a doctor can decide that, after an evaluation. Some have approvals for weight management under certain conditions, but eligibility, risks, and appropriateness are determined strictly individually, in a medical consultation — not based on an article or a clip.

### Can they be bought without a prescription?
No. They are prescription-only medications. Obtaining them from unofficial sources, without evaluation and medical supervision, is risky and strongly inadvisable.

### Does weight loss achieved this way last forever?
This is a frequently discussed topic publicly, but the answer depends on each case and is a question for a doctor. What we can say, neutrally, is that specialists always emphasise the role of healthy long-term habits, regardless of treatment.

### Why don't you say whether they work or not?
Because evaluating efficacy and risks is a matter of medicine, not an informational website. Our position is simple: we inform you that these medications exist and are being discussed, and direct you to the only correct source of decision-making — a specialist.

## The conclusion, simply

These medications **exist** and are a real topic of discussion. That's all. If you're interested in whether they might have any role in your situation, the only correct step is a **conversation with a specialist** (GP, diabetologist, endocrinologist, nutritionist). They can evaluate, can explain risks and benefits, and can make a safe decision — things that an informational website cannot and should not do.
