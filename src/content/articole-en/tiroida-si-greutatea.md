"I have a thyroid problem" is among the most common explanations people give for difficulty
losing weight — and, unlike many other popular excuses, it has a real basis. But how big is the
effect exactly, and how much extra weight can correctly be attributed to the thyroid gland
rather than other factors? The answer is more nuanced than "my thyroid makes me fat."

## What the thyroid does, briefly

The thyroid gland produces hormones (T3 and T4) that regulate the metabolic rate of nearly every
cell in the body. When the thyroid functions below normal — hypothyroidism — basal metabolism
(how many calories you burn at rest) drops, sometimes significantly.

Hypothyroidism can be caused by Hashimoto's thyroiditis (the most common cause, an autoimmune
condition), surgery, radioactive iodine treatment, iodine deficiency, or congenital
dysfunctions.

## How many kilograms, really, hypothyroidism causes

This is where most people overestimate the effect. Clinical studies show untreated
hypothyroidism causes, on average, a weight increase of **2-5 kg**, most of it from water and
salt retention, not pure fat. Severe, long-untreated cases can add more, but 15-20 kg increases
attributed exclusively to the thyroid are rare and usually involve other factors too (reduced
activity from fatigue, dietary changes).

Once hypothyroidism is properly treated with medication (levothyroxine), most of this weight —
especially the water component — is lost relatively quickly, within weeks to a few months.

## Why hypothyroidism makes weight loss harder, even if it doesn't explain all the weight

**Slower basal metabolism** means you need fewer calories than a similar person with normal
thyroid function — so a "standard" eating plan can leave you in a smaller deficit than you think.

**Chronic fatigue**, a classic hypothyroidism symptom, dramatically reduces
[NEAT](/en/articole/neat-miscare-non-sport/) (unplanned daily movement) and exercise motivation —
not from laziness, but from a real physiological state of low energy.

**Water retention** can mask real progress on the scale, even if you're losing fat.

**Appetite** can be indirectly affected through effects on mood and energy — many people with
hypothyroidism report increased cravings for carbohydrates in response to chronic fatigue,
similar to mechanisms in [emotional eating](/en/articole/mancat-emotional/).

## How to know if your thyroid is the problem

The only certain way is a blood test — TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone), and if abnormal, free
T3 and T4. Suggestive symptoms include: persistent fatigue, constant feeling of cold, dry skin,
thinning hair, constipation, difficulty concentrating, and, yes, difficulty losing weight despite
effort.

If you have these symptoms and haven't been tested, it's worth a doctor's visit — a simple test
can quickly clarify whether the thyroid is or isn't part of the equation.

## How to lose weight with hypothyroidism (properly treated)

**Medical treatment comes first.** If TSH is elevated, no diet fully compensates for an untreated
hormonal imbalance. Replacement medication (levothyroxine) needs to be adjusted by a doctor to
the optimal level — this can take several months of titration.

**Once hormonally stabilized, weight loss principles remain the same** as for anyone else —
sustained [caloric deficit](/en/calculator-calorii/), adequate protein
([1.6-2.0 g/kg](/en/articole/proteina-cat-ai-nevoie/) for those dieting), and
[strength training](/en/articole/antrenament-greutati-femei/) to protect muscle mass. There's no
magic "thyroid diet" different from general principles.

**Expect slightly slower progress** than someone without hypothyroidism, due to slightly reduced
basal metabolism even with optimal medication. It's not a reason to give up, just a reason to be
patient with the pace.

**Iodine and selenium matter for thyroid health**, but excessive iodine supplementation can
worsen some forms of autoimmune hypothyroidism (Hashimoto's). Don't supplement without a
doctor's recommendation.

## Common myths about thyroid and weight

**"I have thyroid issues, so I can never lose weight."** False. With proper treatment and solid
dietary principles, people with hypothyroidism do lose weight — just somewhat slower than
average.

**"Gluten-free diets cure Hashimoto's thyroiditis."** There's no solid evidence that eliminating
gluten treats Hashimoto's in people without associated celiac disease. Some people report feeling
better, possibly for other reasons (reducing ultra-processed foods), but it's not a proven
treatment.

**"Natural thyroid supplements can replace medication."** Dangerous and false. Confirmed
hypothyroidism requires prescribed medication, monitored through periodic bloodwork.

## Frequently asked questions

### Does hyperthyroidism (overactive thyroid) make you lose weight?
Yes, usually — increased metabolism burns more calories, which can lead to involuntary weight
loss, often accompanied by other symptoms (palpitations, anxiety, excessive sweating). It
requires just as much medical treatment as hypothyroidism.

### How long after starting treatment does weight normalize?
Water retention is usually lost within weeks of hormonal stabilization. Actual fat requires the
same caloric deficit process as for anyone else.

### Should I get thyroid tests if I can't lose weight?
If you also have other suggestive symptoms (fatigue, cold, thinning hair) and haven't been
tested recently, it's worth discussing with a doctor. If you're losing weight slowly without
other symptoms, the cause is more likely related to actual caloric intake, sleep, or stress.

### Can chronic stress affect the thyroid?
Yes, indirectly — chronically high stress can influence thyroid hormone conversion and can
worsen autoimmune conditions like Hashimoto's, though the exact mechanism is complex and
actively studied.

The thyroid matters, but it's not a complete explanation and not an excuse to give up. With
proper medical treatment and solid basic principles, weight loss remains possible — just with a
bit more patience.
