You go online to find out how to lose weight and, within ten minutes, you're bombarded: pills that "melt fat while you sleep," patches that "dissolve kilograms," "detox" teas, capsules "100% natural" with impressive before-and-after photos. This industry is worth billions precisely because it sells exactly what everyone wants: results without effort. Before you spend a single penny on such products, it's worth understanding what you're actually buying — because in the vast majority of cases, you're buying an expensive illusion, and sometimes an actual risk.

This article sells nothing and recommends no product. It simply shows you how things stand, so you can make an informed decision.

## "Natural" doesn't mean "effective" and doesn't mean "safe"

The most powerful word in the marketing of these products is "natural." It sounds harmless, healthy, trustworthy. The reality is that "natural" says absolutely nothing about efficacy or safety. Many natural substances are completely inactive for weight loss; others are very active — and precisely because of that, they can have side effects or interactions with medications.

Another aspect that ads hide: supplements **are not regulated the same way as medications**. This means nobody rigorously checks whether the capsule contains what the label says it does, whether the substance is pure, or whether the product does what it promises. Two jars on the same shelf can contain different amounts. Essentially, you're paying on blind faith.

## What the most common categories actually do

Let's go through them one by one, because the underlying mechanisms are more mundane than the before-and-after photos suggest.

**"Fat burners."** Usually contain caffeine and stimulants. They may very slightly increase metabolism and temporarily reduce appetite — a small effect, comparable to a strong coffee, nothing like "burning" kilograms. On the other hand, they can cause palpitations, anxiety, and insomnia in sensitive individuals.

**Pills "that suppress appetite."** Some contain fibre (see [glucomannan](/articole/berberina-glucomannan-slabit/)), which does create a feeling of fullness — but a bowl of soup or a portion of beans does the same thing, much more cheaply. Others contain stimulants or substances with unclear effects.

**Teas and "detoxes."** They work through diuretic or laxative effects — meaning you eliminate water and "empty out." The scale temporarily drops, but it's [water, not fat](/articole/ceaiuri-de-slabit/), and it comes straight back.

**Slimming patches.** The idea that a substance applied to the skin "melts" fat underneath has no real basis. Fat is not eliminated locally and doesn't "exit" through the skin. It's essentially a product that relies entirely on the placebo effect.

## The before-and-after myth and how to recognise a dishonest ad

Before-and-after photos are the main weapon. The problem is they prove nothing: they can be different people, photos taken at different times and angles, or people who lost weight through diet and exercise, not the product being promoted. A real transformation can't be attributed to a capsule just because someone was taking it at the time.

Here are the red flags that tell you, almost certainly, you're looking at a scam:

- It promises a number and a timeframe ("minus 10 kg in 2 weeks").
- It says it works "without diet and without exercise."
- It uses words like "miracle," "the secret doctors don't want you to know," "revolutionary."
- It has emotional testimonials but zero serious studies.
- It creates urgency: "today only," "last stocks," "limited offer."
- It doesn't clearly list ingredients and quantities.

If it ticks two or three of these boxes, keep your money.

## What actually works (and it's free)

The frustrating part for the industry is that the things that genuinely produce weight loss can't be packaged and sold expensively: a realistic [calorie deficit](/dieta/deficit-caloric/), protein and fibre from real food (beans, lentils, eggs, cottage cheese, vegetables), regular movement, good sleep, and patience. A few raw carrots curb hunger just as well as a pill, and they give you vitamins too.

If you still want to spend on something that helps with weight loss, a kitchen scale (under £15) and a pair of trainers will do you a thousand times more good than any "fat burner" jar.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is there a weight loss pill that actually works?
No over-the-counter supplement produces significant weight loss on its own. There are prescription medications for certain cases, but those are prescribed and monitored exclusively by a doctor — they're not products you buy yourself. See also the article on [injectable medications](/articole/medicamente-injectabile-slabit/).

### Are weight loss supplements dangerous?
They can be. Some contain stimulants that affect the heart or sleep, others interact with medications, and because they're not strictly regulated, you don't always know exactly what you're taking. With any product, especially if you're on medication, ask a doctor or pharmacist.

### Why do they sell so well if they don't work?
Because they sell hope, not results. The placebo effect, the temporary water loss, and before-and-after photos create the impression that "it works," and aggressive marketing does the rest. Real people's frustration with diets is fertile ground for false promises.

### What about "celebrity-endorsed" products?
A paid endorsement is not scientific evidence. The fact that a well-known person promotes a product only tells you they were paid to do so — not that the product works.

### How do I know if a product is a scam?
Check three things. First, whether it promises a number and a timeframe ("minus 10 kg in 2 weeks") or says "without diet and without exercise" — definite red flag. Second, whether it doesn't clearly list ingredients and quantities on the label. Third, whether it uses artificial urgency ("last stocks") and emotional testimonials instead of concrete information. A serious product doesn't need pressure and spectacular promises to sell.

### Are fibre supplements (glucomannan) an exception?
They have a somewhat more real basis, because fibre does provide satiety — but even there, the effect is small and conditional on a lower-calorie diet. The same satiety comes from a bowl of soup or a portion of beans, at a fraction of the price. The supplement can at best be an aid, not a solution.

The conclusion is simple and saves you wasted money: if a product promises to do the work for you, it's lying. Real weight loss is boring, cheap, and in your hands — less on the plate, more movement, patience. Everything else is expensive packaging.
