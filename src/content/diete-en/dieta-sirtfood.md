The **Sirtfood Diet** is a dietary approach created by British nutritional consultants Aidan
Goggins and Glen Matten, based on the idea of consuming foods rich in **polyphenols** that
activate **sirtuins** — intracellular proteins linked to metabolism, inflammation and longevity.

The diet achieved extraordinary media attention after being associated with Adele's weight
loss transformation in 2020. It consists of **two phases**: Phase 1 (3 days at 1,000 kcal,
4 days at 1,500 kcal — green juices + one meal per day) and Phase 2 (2 weeks, three sirtfood
meals + one juice per day). Maintenance uses sirtfoods as the nutritional basis.

The scientific position: sirtuin research is **real but preliminary**, especially in humans.
Rapid Phase 1 weight loss is largely water and glycogen. The **20 key sirtfoods** — kale,
rocket, dark chocolate, red wine, olive oil, coffee, blueberries and others — are genuinely
nutritious foods rich in antioxidants, regardless of the sirtuin mechanism.

## History and origin

Aidan Goggins and Glen Matten, both British nutritional consultants, developed the Sirtfood
concept after reviewing academic literature on sirtuins — proteins discovered in the early
2000s whose links to caloric restriction and longevity in animal models generated significant
scientific interest. Notable researchers in the field include David Sinclair at Harvard, whose
work on sirtuins and resveratrol was widely publicised.

Goggins and Matten first applied the concept at the KX Gym in Chelsea, London, testing the
protocol on 40 clients over a pilot study. They published the results — an average weight loss
of 7 lb in 7 days — in their book *The Sirtfood Diet* (2016). The claims were not peer-reviewed
clinical trials, but the book sold well and attracted celebrity interest.

The global explosion came in 2020 when Adele was photographed dramatically slimmer and media
reports attributed her transformation to the Sirtfood Diet. The book immediately jumped to the
top of bestseller lists in multiple countries, and demand was so high that some publishers ran
out of copies. The media coverage far exceeded what the scientific evidence warranted, a pattern
typical of celebrity-associated diets in the social-media era.
