**Noom** is a commercial **digital weight-management programme** that distinguishes itself
from classical diets through a **psychological approach**: instead of simply restricting
foods, it uses **cognitive-behavioural therapy (CBT)** principles to help users understand
and change the habits, thoughts and emotions that underlie their eating behaviours.

In practice, the programme provides: a personalised daily calorie goal, a colour-coded food
system (green/yellow/red based on caloric density), educational content on psychology and
nutrition, food and activity logging, and access to a coaching team.

No food is completely forbidden — the philosophy is **awareness** and **conscious choice**,
not prohibition. This approach makes Noom more flexible than traditional diet programmes,
but also less structured for people who prefer clear rules.

## History and origin

Noom was founded in New York in 2008 by Saeju Jeong and Artem Petakov, Korean and Ukrainian
entrepreneurs respectively. The initial idea was to use mobile technology and psychology to
create a weight management programme accessible to anyone with a smartphone, without the
need for in-person consultations or physical support groups.

The company positioned itself from the start as science-based, collaborating with
researchers from Cornell University and Columbia University to develop content and evaluate
the programme. The core technology draws on decades of academic research in cognitive
behavioural therapy for obesity, particularly the work of Aaron Beck and the application
of CBT to eating disorders and weight management.

Noom expanded rapidly in the 2010s in the United States, particularly following an aggressive
digital marketing campaign that prominently featured testimonials. By 2021, the company was
reported to have over 50 million users and a valuation of $3.7 billion, positioning it for
an IPO. The programme has attracted criticism for aggressive retention practices and
subscription cancellation difficulties, generating formal complaints to consumer-protection
agencies. Despite this, it remains one of the best-known digital weight management programmes
globally, reflecting a broader shift from traditional diet books towards app-based coaching
and behavioural interventions.
