The **Zone Diet** was developed by American biochemist Dr Barry Sears and prescribes a
specific macronutrient ratio at **every meal**: **40% carbohydrates, 30% protein and 30%
fat**. The goal is to maintain stable insulin levels and promote an anti-inflammatory hormonal
state — the "Zone".

The approach is more **prescriptive** than most balanced diets: not just a general direction
but a precise ratio enforced at every eating occasion, including snacks. This makes it
effective for people who want a clear structure, but demanding to follow consistently.

The macronutrient split reduces refined carbohydrates and increases protein compared to
standard dietary guidelines, principles that are broadly supported by nutritional research,
even if the specific 40/30/30 target and "Zone" mechanism are more Sears's interpretation
than established science.

## History and origin

Barry Sears is an American biochemist who spent years at MIT and Boston University studying
lipids and eicosanoids — hormones derived from fatty acids that regulate inflammation. He
developed the hypothesis that diet-mediated control of eicosanoids — achievable through the
40/30/30 macronutrient ratio — was the key to optimal health and body composition.

He published *Enter the Zone* (1995), which became one of the bestselling diet books of the
1990s, selling over 2 million copies in the United States and being translated into dozens
of languages. The book positioned the Zone Diet as a solution not just for weight loss but
for peak athletic performance and chronic disease prevention — an ambitious claim that
generated both enthusiasm and scepticism from the medical community.

The Zone Diet attracted a significant celebrity and athlete following in the late 1990s,
particularly in Hollywood and among professional athletes. Its profile in the fitness world
was boosted in the 2000s when CrossFit founder Greg Glassman officially recommended it as
the ideal dietary complement to CrossFit training, pairing it with the Paleo diet as the
two foundational nutrition approaches for CrossFit athletes. This association embedded the
Zone Diet in the CrossFit community for years.

Sears has continued publishing and updating the Zone concept, incorporating newer research
on omega-3 fatty acids and polyphenols into what he calls the "Zone 2.0" framework. The
core 40/30/30 principle remains unchanged.
