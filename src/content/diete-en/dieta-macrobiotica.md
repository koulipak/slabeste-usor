The macrobiotic diet is a dietary approach with strong philosophical roots drawn from Japanese
and Zen Buddhist traditions. It is based on **whole grains** (50–60% of meals), supplemented
with **cooked vegetables**, **legumes** and **seaweed**, with an emphasis on local, seasonal
and minimally processed foods.

Beyond diet, macrobiotics incorporates principles of balance between opposing forces (yin and
yang) applied to foods and the body — a framework that has no direct equivalent in modern
nutritional science but shapes the recommendations.

Nutritionally, macrobiotics is associated with high fibre intake and low saturated fat, but
presents **significant risks of deficiency** in vitamin B12, calcium, vitamin D and iron —
especially in the strict variant that eliminates dairy, eggs and most animal products. It is
not recommended for pregnant women or children without expert supervision.

## History and origin

Macrobiotics in its modern form was developed by Japanese philosopher George Ohsawa
(Nyoichi Sakurazawa, 1893–1966), who built on the theories of Japanese doctor Sagen Ishizuka
from the late nineteenth century. Ohsawa combined traditional Japanese dietary principles
with a yin-yang philosophical framework, claiming that a diet based on whole grains and
vegetables could prevent and treat various diseases, including cancer. He published dozens of
books and lectured extensively in Japan and Europe.

The Western spread of macrobiotics came through Michio Kushi, one of Ohsawa's students, who
moved to the United States in 1949 and built the Kushi Institute in Boston, which became the
centre of the American macrobiotic movement. Kushi and his wife Aveline promoted macrobiotics
through books, seminars and a network of centres. *The Book of Macrobiotics* (1977) became
an international bestseller and reached readers worldwide, including Romania after 1989.

The macrobiotic diet attracted controversy when Kushi and others claimed it could treat and
cure cancer — claims that generated investigations by health authorities and were condemned
by the American Cancer Society and other organisations. Despite this, the diet continued to
attract followers seeking alternatives to conventional medicine or a structured dietary
philosophy. Today it is followed mainly as a lifestyle by a niche audience interested in
traditional Japanese culture and holistic philosophy.
