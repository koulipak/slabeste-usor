**Whole30** is a strict **30-day elimination programme** developed by Melissa and Dallas
Hartwig in 2009. It removes **sugar** (all forms), **grains**, **dairy**, **legumes** and
**alcohol** for 30 complete days, then reintroduces them systematically one category at a
time to identify which foods cause problems for the individual.

The programme frames itself not as a diet but as a **health reset** — 30 days of eating
whole, minimally processed foods to break habits, reduce inflammation and identify personal
food sensitivities. Weighing yourself during the 30 days is discouraged.

A notable rule: no recreating eliminated foods with compliant ingredients ("Whole30
pancakes"). The founders argue that this perpetuates the psychological dependence on these
foods that the programme is meant to break.

## History and origin

Melissa Hartwig Urban and her then-husband Dallas Hartwig developed Whole30 in 2009, initially
as a personal experiment and blog post. Melissa, a sports nutritionist, and Dallas, a physical
therapist, began sharing the protocol online and it attracted immediate interest. The programme
spread organically through social media — particularly Instagram and dedicated Facebook groups
— years before influencer marketing became a standard commercial strategy.

The book *It Starts With Food* (2012) provided the scientific rationale and context for the
programme, drawing on paleo, anti-inflammatory and gut-health research. *The Whole30: The
30-Day Guide to Total Health and Food Freedom* (2015) became a New York Times bestseller
and sold over a million copies, with multiple follow-up books in the series.

The programme's viral growth was driven by community: the Whole30 community on social media
generated millions of posts, transformation photos and daily accountability threads. Melissa
Hartwig Urban became a prominent voice in the food freedom and clean-eating space. The
programme has attracted criticism for its restrictive rules, the lack of independent clinical
research, and the elimination of nutritious foods (legumes, dairy), but retains a large global
following as a structured short-term habit reset.
