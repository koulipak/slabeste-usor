The low-carb diet reduces carbohydrate intake below **100–150 g per day** (sometimes less),
replacing them with protein and healthy fat. It is a flexible category — it includes
everything from moderate carbohydrate restriction to strict approaches like keto.

The mechanism: reducing carbohydrates lowers insulin levels, which facilitates fat mobilisation
and reduces appetite. Studies show that low-carb diets produce faster initial weight loss than
low-fat diets, though the difference in the long term is more modest.

Unlike keto, low-carb **does not require entering ketosis** — it is sufficient to reduce
carbohydrates enough to improve blood glucose and insulin without reaching the strict threshold
of 20–50 g per day. This makes it more flexible and easier to follow in social situations.

## History and origin

Carbohydrate restriction as a weight-loss strategy is much older than keto or Atkins. William
Banting, a British undertaker, published *Letter on Corpulence* (1863) — one of the first
popular texts on dietary weight loss — which described how he lost significant weight by
eliminating sugar, starchy foods and beer, under the advice of his physician William Harvey.
This protocol was for decades called the "Banting diet" in the UK and Northern Europe.

In the early twentieth century, attention to carbohydrates fluctuated as low-fat diet theory
gained ground in the 1950s–1970s, associated with the work of Ancel Keys. The research of
John Yudkin (*Pure, White and Deadly*, 1972) argued for sugar's role in heart disease but
was overshadowed by Keys' more influential work for several decades.

The resurgence of low-carb came through Robert Atkins in the 1970s and 1990s, and through
research published in major journals in the early 2000s (Gardner 2007, comparing Atkins with
LEARN, Ornish and Zone, finding advantages for Atkins in short-term weight loss). The PURE
study (2017), published in *The Lancet*, found an association between high carbohydrate intake
and mortality in international populations, contributing to the reassessment of carbohydrate
restriction in nutritional guidelines.
