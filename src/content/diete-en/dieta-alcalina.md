The alkaline diet starts from the idea that foods can influence the body's internal **pH**
and that a more alkaline environment would be healthier. According to this theory, foods are
divided into "acid-forming" (meat, dairy, sugar, alcohol) and "alkalising" (vegetables, fruit,
legumes).

**From a clinical standpoint**, the premise is not supported by solid evidence — the human
body regulates blood pH precisely through the lungs and kidneys, independent of diet.
However, the alkaline diet promotes foods that are recognised as beneficial for other reasons:
rich in fibre, antioxidants and nutrients, with low caloric density.

In practice, following the alkaline diet means **more vegetables, fruit and legumes** and
less processed meat, sugar and ultra-processed foods — a broadly healthy pattern, regardless
of the pH theory that underpins it.

## History and origin

The theoretical origins of the alkaline diet can be found in the work of American physician
William Howard Hay, who in the 1920s argued that "acid-forming" foods were the primary source
of modern disease and that a diet based on "alkalising" foods could restore health. Hay was
not a nutritionist in the modern sense, and his theory did not withstand later clinical
scrutiny, but it influenced generations of followers of alternative medicine.

The concept was revived and modernised in the 1990s–2000s, most prominently by Robert O.
Young, who published *The pH Miracle* in 2002. The book sold millions of copies and
transformed the alkaline diet from a fringe theory into a global wellness trend. Young
claimed that internal acidity causes cancer and other serious diseases — claims for which he
was convicted in the USA in 2017 for practising medicine illegally.

Despite the lack of validation for the central mechanism, the alkaline diet remains popular,
partly because the foods it promotes — vegetables, fruit, legumes — are recognised by
conventional nutrition as beneficial. The medical community agrees with the practical
recommendations but not with the pseudoscientific premise of modifying blood pH through diet.
