The **South Beach Diet** is a three-phase low-carb dietary approach developed by Miami
cardiologist Arthur Agatston in the late 1990s. Unlike Atkins, which focuses purely on
carbohydrate restriction, South Beach also distinguishes **good fats from bad**: it
encourages unsaturated fats (olive oil, avocado, nuts) while limiting saturated and trans fats.

The protocol: **Phase 1** (2 weeks, strict) eliminates all refined carbohydrates, sugar,
fruit and alcohol — rapid initial weight loss. **Phase 2** gradually reintroduces whole
grains, legumes and most fruit — continued gradual loss. **Phase 3** is the lifelong
maintenance phase with all food groups included in appropriate proportions.

The diet was designed with **cardiovascular health** as a primary concern, not just weight
loss — a distinction from many commercial diets.

## History and origin

Arthur Agatston developed the South Beach Diet in the late 1990s while working as a
preventive cardiologist in Miami, initially to help his patients improve cardiovascular
markers. Frustrated with the low-fat dietary advice prevailing at the time, which he felt
wasn't working for his patients, he began developing a protocol based on glycaemic index
research and the emerging science distinguishing healthy from unhealthy fats.

He published *The South Beach Diet* in 2003. The book became one of the bestselling diet
books in American history — over 23 million copies sold worldwide in multiple languages.
The "South Beach" name evoked the glamorous, health-conscious Miami Beach aesthetic and
resonated with readers in a way that a purely clinical title wouldn't have.

Agatston is also known for developing the **Agatston calcium score** — a cardiac CT
measurement for coronary artery calcification that has become a standard tool in
cardiovascular risk assessment. This clinical credibility differentiated him from popular
diet-book authors without medical backgrounds and gave the South Beach Diet a degree of
institutional credibility unusual in the diet book market.

The diet's popularity peaked in the early to mid-2000s but it retains a following and has
influenced subsequent low-carb approaches that, like South Beach, emphasise carbohydrate
quality rather than blanket elimination.
