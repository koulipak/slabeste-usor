The **NALEP diet** (No Added Liquid Energy Programme) is one of the simplest dietary
interventions: **eliminate all caloric beverages** — sweetened drinks, juices, alcohol,
sweetened coffee — and replace them with water, unsweetened tea or coffee. No food
restrictions whatsoever.

The scientific basis is solid: **liquid calories are poorly compensated** — the body does
not reduce solid food intake proportionally after drinking calories, unlike after eating them.
A person drinking 2–3 sweetened beverages daily can easily eliminate 400–700 kcal per day
from this change alone, without any other dietary modification.

For people whose diet contains significant liquid calories, NALEP is one of the most
**effective and sustainable** dietary changes available — a single clear rule that requires
no calorie counting, no food elimination and no meal planning.

## History and origin

The NALEP term was introduced and popularised in Romania mainly through the work of Dr
Mihaela Bilic, one of the most prominent Romanian nutritionists, who used it in public
communication to describe the principle of eliminating liquid calories as a standalone
weight-loss strategy. In the international literature, the same concept is referred to as
"reducing liquid calorie intake" or "no caloric beverages", without a standard acronym.

The scientific evidence underlying NALEP is well established. Research by Richard Mattes and
colleagues at Purdue University, published from the early 2000s onwards, systematically
demonstrated that calories consumed in liquid form produce significantly lower satiety
responses than the same calories consumed in solid form. Studies specifically on sugar-sweetened
beverages consistently show their association with weight gain and obesity, independently of
other dietary factors.

Epidemiological data from large cohorts (including the Nurses' Health Study and the Health
Professionals Follow-up Study at Harvard) showed that each additional daily serving of a
sugar-sweetened beverage was associated with a meaningful increase in weight gain over time.
These findings have influenced public health policy: multiple countries have introduced sugar
taxes on sweetened beverages, and WHO guidelines explicitly recommend limiting sugar intake
from these sources.

In Romania, Dr Bilic's communication work brought the NALEP concept to a wide audience
through television appearances, books and social media, making it one of the most searched
dietary terms in Romanian. Its appeal: a single rule, applicable immediately, without any
other lifestyle change required.
