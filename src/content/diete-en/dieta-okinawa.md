The **Okinawa diet** is the traditional eating pattern of the inhabitants of Okinawa, a
Japanese island that has long been one of the world's **Blue Zones** — regions with an
exceptional concentration of centenarians and very low rates of chronic disease. It is not
a commercial diet programme but a documented cultural eating pattern.

The traditional base: **sweet potatoes** (historically ~67% of caloric intake), supplemented
with vegetables, tofu, seaweed, miso and occasional fish. Red meat was very rare, dairy
practically absent. A key cultural principle is **hara hachi bu** — stop eating at 80%
satiety, not 100%.

It is important to note that the **traditional Okinawa diet is historical**, not contemporary.
After the Second World War and especially since the 1960s, Westernisation of the food supply
radically changed what Okinawans actually eat today. Longevity data are from generations
who ate in the traditional way.

## History and origin

Scientific interest in the longevity of the Okinawan population began in the 1970s–1980s,
when Japanese public health researchers began documenting the exceptionally high number of
centenarians in this island group. The Okinawa Centenarian Study, launched in 1975 by
Makoto Suzuki — a gerontologist at the University of the Ryukyus — collected detailed data
on the genetics, lifestyle and diet of more than 900 centenarians over several decades.

Suzuki's findings were brought to an international audience through the book *The Okinawa
Program* (2001), co-authored with Bradley and Craig Willcox, which described the diet,
physical activity, social connections and purpose (ikigai) of long-lived Okinawans.
The book sold widely and generated interest in Blue Zone research, later systematised by
journalist Dan Buettner in collaboration with National Geographic.

Buettner's 2005 article in *National Geographic* and his subsequent books (*The Blue Zones*,
2008) popularised the concept of Blue Zones — Okinawa, Sardinia, Nicoya (Costa Rica), Icaria
(Greece) and Loma Linda (California) — as natural experiments on longevity. The Okinawa diet
became one of the most studied traditional dietary patterns alongside the Mediterranean diet.
Epidemiological data on traditional Okinawans remains one of the strongest natural arguments
for a plant-centred diet low in saturated fat and calories.
