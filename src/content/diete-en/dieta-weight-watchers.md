**Weight Watchers** (rebranded as **WW** in 2018) is one of the oldest and most extensively
researched commercial weight-management programmes, operating since 1963. It uses a
**points system** (currently PersonalPoints) in which every food has a value based on its
nutritional content, and members work within a personalised daily points budget.

The key feature: **ZeroPoint foods** — vegetables, fruit, eggs, chicken breast, fish,
legumes and others (personalised by health profile) — can be eaten without tracking, directing
members naturally towards nutritious choices. No food is completely forbidden.

The programme combines the **points system** with **community support** (weekly group
meetings or online check-ins) and accountability — factors that research identifies as
central to long-term weight-loss success. Multiple independent clinical studies confirm
WW's effectiveness, distinguishing it from many commercial diet programmes without a
research base.

## History and origin

Weight Watchers was founded in 1963 by Jean Nidetch, a New York housewife who had struggled
with her weight for years. After losing weight with a programme from the New York City
Department of Health, she began inviting friends to her home to share strategies and mutual
support. She recognised that the group format — shared accountability and communal problem
solving — was essential to success in a way that individual dieting wasn't.

Nidetch formalised the approach and, with business partners Felice and Al Lippert, launched
Weight Watchers as a commercial company in 1963. The first formal meeting attracted 400
people. The company grew rapidly throughout the 1960s–1970s, expanding internationally.
Nidetch became the public face of the movement and published her autobiography, *The Story
of Weight Watchers* (1970).

The company was sold to H.J. Heinz in 1978, then to Artal Luxembourg in 1999, before going
public on the New York Stock Exchange in 2001. In 2015, Oprah Winfrey acquired a 10% stake
and became a spokesperson — a move that sent the company's shares up 170% in a single day
and brought a new wave of media attention. The 2018 rebrand to WW reflected attempts to
modernise the brand and address criticism that the weight-focused name was stigmatising.
Today WW operates in dozens of countries with millions of members and a digital-first model.
