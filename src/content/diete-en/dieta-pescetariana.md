The **pescatarian diet** (or pescetarian) is a vegetarian-based approach that **includes
fish and seafood**, while excluding meat (beef, pork, lamb) and poultry. It combines the
benefits of a plant-based diet — high in fibre, antioxidants and low in saturated fat —
with the nutritional richness of fish, particularly **omega-3 fatty acids**.

Unlike veganism or strict vegetarianism, the pescatarian diet is **nutritionally complete**
without requiring supplementation: fish, dairy and eggs cover vitamin B12, omega-3,
calcium and iron — nutrients that can be deficient on a vegan diet.

It is considered one of the most **practical and sustainable** of the partially plant-based
diets: flexible, socially easy to follow (fish is available in almost any restaurant),
and associated with better cardiovascular and metabolic health compared to omnivore diets.

## History and origin

The term "pescatarian" (from Italian *pesce*, fish, and vegetarian) was used from at least
the early 1990s in the English-speaking world, though its exact origin is unclear. The
concept of eating fish but not meat has existed in various cultural traditions for centuries
— many Catholic countries practised it on Fridays, and numerous Buddhist and Hindu
communities have long incorporated fish while avoiding red meat.

Scientific interest in the pescatarian approach grew alongside the accumulation of
evidence for the health benefits of the Mediterranean diet — which naturally includes fish —
and the growing body of research on omega-3 fatty acids. The EPIC (European Prospective
Investigation into Cancer and Nutrition) study, which followed hundreds of thousands of
participants across Europe, provided significant data on the health outcomes of pescatarians
compared to omnivores, vegetarians and vegans, generally showing favourable outcomes for
fish-eaters.

Today the pescatarian diet is one of the most popular semi-vegetarian approaches, especially
in Western countries. It has been adopted by many people as a middle ground between the
ethical and environmental motivations of vegetarianism and the nutritional convenience of an
omnivore diet.
