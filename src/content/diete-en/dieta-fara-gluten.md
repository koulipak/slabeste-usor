The gluten-free diet eliminates **gluten** — a protein complex found in wheat, rye and barley —
from all foods. For people with **coeliac disease**, it is not a lifestyle choice but a
**medical necessity**: even trace amounts of gluten trigger an autoimmune reaction that
damages the intestinal mucosa and impairs nutrient absorption.

For people **without coeliac disease** — who do not have non-coeliac gluten sensitivity
either — clinical evidence does not support the idea that eliminating gluten brings health
benefits or helps with weight loss. Many people perceive improvement after going gluten-free,
but this is most likely attributable to eliminating processed foods and paying more attention
to nutrition in general, not to the absence of gluten itself.

Used electively (without medical necessity), the gluten-free diet presents the risk of
nutritional deficiencies if not carefully planned, and gluten-free commercial products are
often more expensive and contain more sugar and fat than their conventional counterparts.

## History and origin

The connection between gluten and coeliac disease was established through pioneering work by
Dutch paediatrician Willem Karel Dicke. During the Second World War, he observed that
children with coeliac disease improved when wheat bread disappeared from the Netherlands due
to food shortages, and deteriorated again when it returned after the war. He published his
findings in 1950, and this observation became the basis of the strict gluten-free diet as the
treatment for coeliac disease.

For decades, the gluten-free diet remained a purely medical protocol, followed almost
exclusively by people with diagnosed coeliac disease. The transformation into a popular
wellness trend occurred in the 2000s, driven by several factors: books like *Wheat Belly*
by William Davis (2011) and *Grain Brain* by David Perlmutter (2013) attributed a range of
health problems — from obesity to neurological conditions — to wheat and gluten, without
clinical evidence at the level of general recommendations.

Celebrity endorsements and a strong social-media presence transformed the gluten-free diet
into one of the most popular food trends of the 2010s. The market for gluten-free products
exploded globally — in the US it exceeded $15 billion by 2020 — while surveys showed that
the majority of consumers of these products did not have coeliac disease. This phenomenon
prompted numerous studies investigating the alleged benefits of gluten elimination for the
general population, which did not confirm the claims of popular books.
