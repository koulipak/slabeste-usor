The Mediterranean diet is not a strict slimming diet but a **dietary pattern** inspired by
the eating habits of countries around the Mediterranean Sea. At its core are vegetables,
fruit, fish, legumes, whole grains and **olive oil**, while red meat and sweets appear rarely.

For people who want to lose weight without eliminating entire food groups, it is one of the
most sustainable choices: varied, balanced and easy to follow for years. Weight loss comes
more slowly than with restrictive diets, but with a far lower risk of regaining the lost weight.

## History and origin

The Mediterranean diet was conceptualised and studied by American nutritionist Ancel Keys
(1904–2004), who launched the famous "Seven Countries Study" in 1958 — a large-scale
epidemiological study that followed cohorts of men from seven countries for twenty years,
correlating dietary habits with the incidence of cardiovascular disease. Keys observed that
populations around the Mediterranean basin — especially in Crete, southern Italy and Greece —
had far lower rates of heart disease than the USA or Finland, and attributed this difference
to the consumption of olive oil, fish, vegetables and whole grains. Keys himself adopted the
Mediterranean diet and lived to 100, dying in 2004 at his home in Pioppi, Italy.

The term "Mediterranean diet" was popularised through Keys's publications and those of his
wife Margaret in the 1950s–1970s and gained progressive academic recognition. The PREDIMED
trial (Prevención con Dieta Mediterránea), a Spanish randomised trial published in the
*New England Journal of Medicine* in 2013 and updated in 2018, confirmed that the Mediterranean
diet supplemented with extra-virgin olive oil or nuts significantly reduces the risk of major
cardiovascular events compared with a low-fat control diet.

UNESCO inscribed the Mediterranean diet on the Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2010
(Greece, Spain, Italy, Morocco) and extended recognition in 2013 (Cyprus, Croatia, Portugal).
It is the only diet that benefits from both academic recognition through major clinical trials
and international cultural heritage status. Organisations such as the WHO and FAO cite it as
a reference model for health and sustainability.
