The high-protein diet does not eliminate food groups; it **increases the share of lean
proteins** — meat, fish, eggs, low-fat dairy, legumes — at the expense of refined
carbohydrates and sugar. The idea is that protein satisfies strongly and helps preserve
muscle mass during weight loss.

It is a flexible approach, easily adapted, and especially popular among people who exercise.
Excess should still be avoided in people with kidney problems, and variety matters to prevent
monotony.

## History and origin

High-protein diets do not have a single inventor but crystallised gradually from several
research directions. In the 1970s–1980s, interest in the role of protein in body composition
grew alongside the popularisation of bodybuilding and strength sports, which promoted high
protein intake for building muscle mass. Gradually, researchers began investigating whether
the same principle applied to weight loss.

A key study was published in 2005 by Weigle and colleagues in the *American Journal of
Clinical Nutrition*, demonstrating that a diet with 30% of calories from protein spontaneously
reduces total caloric intake and produces significantly greater satiety than standard diets.
Other studies, including those led by Stuart Phillips at McMaster University, confirmed that
protein helps preserve muscle mass during a caloric deficit, supporting metabolism long-term.

Today, increasing protein intake is recommended in various forms by nutritional guidelines
for weight loss, especially for active individuals. There is no single protocol — recommended
levels range from 1.2 to 2 g per kilogram of body weight depending on goals and activity
level — and the term "high-protein diet" covers a wide range of approaches, from Dukan and
Atkins to balanced diets that prioritise lean proteins.
