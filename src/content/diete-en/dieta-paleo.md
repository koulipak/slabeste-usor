The **Paleo diet** (Paleolithic diet) is based on the idea of eating foods that would have
been available to our Paleolithic ancestors before the advent of agriculture: **meat, fish,
eggs, vegetables, fruit, nuts and seeds**. It eliminates all products of agriculture and
modern food processing: grains, dairy, legumes, refined sugar and processed oils.

The logic is evolutionary: proponents argue that the human body is better adapted to
pre-agricultural foods and that chronic diseases (obesity, diabetes, cardiovascular disease)
emerged with the introduction of agricultural and processed products. This argument is
contested by anthropologists who note that prehistoric diets varied enormously across
regions and populations.

Nutritionally, paleo is a diet **high in protein, fibre and natural fat**, low in refined
carbohydrates. It eliminates some nutritious food groups (dairy, legumes, whole grains) — a
source of criticism for long-term nutritional completeness.

## History and origin

The theoretical basis for a diet inspired by Paleolithic eating was proposed independently
by several researchers in the second half of the twentieth century. Gastroenterologist Walter
Voegtlin argued in *The Stone Age Diet* (1975) that humans are carnivores by nature and that
a meat-based diet is optimal — an extreme position not subsequently endorsed by the
nutritional community.

The first comprehensive academic formulation came in a 1985 article by S. Boyd Eaton and
Melvin Konner in the *New England Journal of Medicine* — one of the most prestigious medical
journals — entitled "Paleolithic Nutrition". Eaton and Konner analysed available
anthropological data and proposed a balanced diet of lean meat, fish, fruit and vegetables,
arguing it was consistent with evolutionary biology. This article gave scientific credibility
to the concept.

The popularisation to a broad audience came through Loren Cordain, an exercise physiology
professor at Colorado State University, who published *The Paleo Diet* in 2002. Cordain drew
on Eaton's work and developed it into a comprehensive protocol, describing the modern paleo
diet as it is commonly known. The book sold over a million copies and sparked a movement.
Robb Wolf, a student of Cordain, further popularised it through *The Paleo Solution* (2010)
and extensive online presence, contributing to the paleo diet's peak of popularity in
the 2010s.
