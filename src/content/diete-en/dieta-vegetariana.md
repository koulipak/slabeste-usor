The **vegetarian diet** excludes all **meat and fish** but includes **dairy and eggs** (in
the most common lacto-ovo variant). All plant foods are allowed: vegetables, fruit, legumes,
whole grains, nuts and seeds.

It is one of the most extensively studied dietary patterns. Large prospective cohort studies
(EPIC, Adventist Health Studies) consistently associate vegetarianism with **lower rates of
cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes and certain cancers**, as well as lower average body
weight, compared to omnivore diets.

Unlike veganism, a lacto-ovo vegetarian diet is **nutritionally complete** without mandatory
supplementation for most healthy adults — dairy and eggs cover vitamin B12, calcium, iron
(partially) and omega-3. Iron and omega-3 still require attention, as plant sources are less
bioavailable.

## History and origin

Organised vegetarianism as a movement began in nineteenth-century England. The Vegetarian
Society was founded in 1847 in Manchester, using the word "vegetarian" for what had
previously been called a "Pythagorean diet" — referencing the ancient Greek philosopher
Pythagoras, who was reported to abstain from meat for ethical reasons.

Religious traditions had prescribed vegetarianism for millennia before the modern movement:
Hinduism, Jainism and Buddhism all have strong vegetarian traditions rooted in the principle
of ahimsa (non-violence towards living beings). In the West, Seventh-day Adventists promoted
vegetarianism as part of their health theology from the mid-nineteenth century; the Adventist
Health Studies, which have followed Adventist communities since the 1950s, have provided some
of the most valuable long-term epidemiological data on vegetarian health outcomes.

In the twentieth century, vegetarianism grew gradually in Western countries, accelerating from
the 1970s with the environmental movement and Peter Singer's *Animal Liberation* (1975). The
1990s–2000s saw significant institutional endorsements: the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics
published its position paper stating that well-planned vegetarian diets are nutritionally
adequate and health-promoting. Today, vegetarianism is mainstream in most Western countries,
with extensive food industry support for meat alternatives.
