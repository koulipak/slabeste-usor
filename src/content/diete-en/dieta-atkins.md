The Atkins diet is one of the best-known **low-carb** diets. It works in **four phases**:
it starts with an induction phase in which carbohydrates are drastically reduced, then
gradually reintroduces them up to a personal maintenance level. The emphasis is on protein
and fat, which provide satiety.

The main advantage is rapid weight loss at the start and the absence of calorie counting.
The disadvantage is the restrictive nature of the first phase and the need to follow the
phases carefully to avoid weight regain.

## History and origin

The Atkins diet was created by American cardiologist Robert C. Atkins, inspired by an article
published in 1963 in the *Journal of the American Medical Association* by Alfred Pennington,
who described the benefits of reducing carbohydrates for weight control. Atkins tested the
approach on himself and his patients, achieving results he presented to the public in
*Dr. Atkins' Diet Revolution* (1972). The book provoked immediate medical controversy but
sold millions of copies and placed carbohydrate restriction at the centre of the public debate
about weight loss.

The diet went through several editions and reformulations. *Dr. Atkins' New Diet Revolution*
(1992) updated the principles and brought a new wave of popularity, coinciding with growing
interest in low-carb diets in the United States. Atkins founded Atkins Nutritionals, a company
producing foods and supplements compatible with his diet. He died in 2003 following an
accident, and the company continued to promote the programme, going through insolvency
proceedings in 2005 before returning to market.

The Atkins diet profoundly influenced modern nutrition: it stimulated academic research into
the effects of carbohydrate restriction and contributed to the emergence of the keto diet,
the Paleo diet and other low-carb variants. Debates about its long-term safety, particularly
regarding saturated fat intake, continue in the specialist literature.
