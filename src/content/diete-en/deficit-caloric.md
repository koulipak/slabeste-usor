Losing weight through a **caloric deficit** is, in fact, the principle behind every diet that
works: you consume fewer calories than you burn. Instead of banning foods, this approach
simply asks you to stay below your daily needs, leaving you free to choose what you eat.

The advantage is total flexibility and the fact that it teaches you about portions and balance.
The disadvantage is the effort of weighing and tracking, especially at the start. You can
estimate your needs with the [calorie calculator](/calculator-calorii/) and begin with a
moderate deficit.

## History and origin

The concept of caloric deficit as the mechanism of weight loss belongs to thermodynamics
applied to nutrition, formalised gradually in the first half of the twentieth century as food
chemistry and digestive physiology advanced. Max Wishnofsky published in 1958 in the
*American Journal of Clinical Nutrition* what became the canonical rule for the following
decades: 3,500 kilocalories equate to approximately 0.45 kg of body fat. This rule dominated
dietary guidelines and medical counselling for more than fifty years.

Researchers in the second half of the twentieth century continually refined the model, adding
variables related to body composition, the type of tissue lost and metabolic adaptation. Kevin
Hall and colleagues at the National Institutes of Health (NIH) developed dynamic equations
accounting for the fact that caloric needs change as weight decreases, explaining why the
same deficit does not produce the same loss over time.

Today, the notion of caloric deficit remains the central principle recognised in nutrition for
weight loss, regardless of the diet applied. Current debate focuses not on the validity of
the deficit as a mechanism, but on the factors influencing long-term adherence: satiety, food
composition, hormones and the food environment.
