The flexitarian diet is a **predominantly plant-based** approach that permits the occasional
inclusion of meat and fish, without strict rules or a complete prohibition on animal products.
The name is a portmanteau of "flexible" and "vegetarian".

The principle: building the majority of meals around vegetables, fruit, legumes and whole
grains, while reducing — but not eliminating — meat. It is the most accessible of the
plant-based diets and the easiest to follow socially and practically.

Research associates the flexitarian diet with **lower body weight**, reduced cardiovascular
risk and improved metabolic markers, compared to a predominantly meat-based diet. It is
considered one of the most sustainable dietary approaches for the long term, precisely because
of its flexibility.

## History and origin

The term "flexitarian" was coined by dietitian Dawn Jackson Blatner, who popularised the concept
in her book *The Flexitarian Diet* (2009). Blatner described a structured approach: on 5 days
per week you eat plant-based, on 2 days you can include meat — a simple and practical formula
that resonated with people attracted to vegetarianism but unwilling to give up meat entirely.

The concept was not entirely new — variations of semi-vegetarian diet had existed for decades —
but Blatner gave it a name, a structure and a popular voice. "Flexitarian" was added to the
Merriam-Webster dictionary in 2012, signalling cultural acceptance of the term.

The diet gained significant popularity in the 2010s alongside broader interest in
plant-based eating, the sustainability movement and growing research on the environmental
and health impacts of meat consumption. Reports from organisations such as the EAT-Lancet
Commission (2019) recommended diets closer to the plant-based model, without demanding total
meat elimination — indirectly validating the flexitarian philosophy. Today the flexitarian
diet consistently ranks among the best dietary approaches in annual expert assessments.
