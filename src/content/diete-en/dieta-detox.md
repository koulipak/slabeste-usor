A **detox diet** refers to a category of short-term restrictive protocols — typically based
on juices, smoothies, broths or raw foods — intended to "cleanse" the body of toxins. In
practice, it is more a general term than a single defined diet.

**The scientific position** is cautious: the liver and kidneys continuously remove metabolic
waste products and environmental substances. There is no rigorous evidence that short-term
juice protocols enhance this natural function in healthy adults. The weight lost is mainly
water and glycogen, not fat.

That said, many people use a detox as a **motivational reset**: a few days of eliminating
alcohol, processed foods and sugar can genuinely improve how they feel and motivate a
transition to healthier long-term habits. From this perspective, the benefit is real — just
not for the reasons usually cited.

## History and origin

The idea of "purifying" the body through restrictive diets has very old roots — in Ayurvedic
medicine, traditional Chinese medicine and Greek medical writings, fasting and purging were
used as therapeutic techniques. The modern concept of "detox diet" as a commercial product
emerged mainly in the United States and the United Kingdom in the 1990s–2000s.

Naturopath Peter Glickman popularised the "Master Cleanse" — lemon juice with maple syrup and
cayenne pepper for 10 days — largely through his 2004 book, although the protocol was first
described by Stanley Burroughs in 1941. The idea reached mainstream attention when celebrities
(including Beyoncé) publicly mentioned using similar protocols. This celebrity endorsement
generated significant media coverage and drove sales of detox products worldwide.

In the 2000s–2010s the market exploded: cold-pressed juice companies, detox teas, activated
charcoal, detox patches and dozens of other products used the term as a marketing claim. At
the same time, scientific examination of these claims began. A 2015 systematic review in the
*Journal of Human Nutrition and Dietetics* found that there were no rigorous clinical trials
showing that commercial detox products improve the body's detoxification processes. Criticism
from nutrition researchers has intensified, and many consumer-protection and public-health
organisations now explicitly caution against the medical claims attached to these products.
