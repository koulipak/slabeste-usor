The **Ornish diet** is a **very-low-fat, almost entirely plant-based** dietary approach
developed by American cardiologist Dr Dean Ornish. Its distinguishing feature: it was
**clinically proven** in controlled studies to **reverse coronary artery disease** — not
merely halt its progression — without medication or surgery.

The protocol: less than 10% of calories from fat (compared to 30–40% in an average Western
diet), no meat, no oils, fat-free dairy only, no egg yolks. The full Ornish programme
integrates **four components**: diet, exercise, stress management and social support.

It is one of the **most restrictive** dietary approaches in existence, and adherence long-term
is very challenging. It was developed for a specific purpose — cardiac reversal — and is not
intended as a general-public diet. The US Medicare system covers it as a medically supervised
cardiac rehabilitation programme.

## History and origin

Dean Ornish began his research in the 1970s as a student at Harvard Medical School and the
University of California, San Francisco (UCSF), inspired by his cardiologist mentor Meyer
Friedman and by meetings with yoga teacher Swami Satchidananda, who influenced his thinking
on the holistic role of lifestyle in disease.

His seminal study — the Lifestyle Heart Trial — was published in *The Lancet* in 1990. For
the first time in a randomised controlled trial, Ornish showed that a combination of a
very-low-fat diet, exercise, stress management and group support led to **measurable reversal
of coronary artery atherosclerosis** at 1 year, confirmed at 5 years. The result was
extraordinary because it challenged the prevailing view that heart disease could only be
slowed, not reversed.

The study, though small (48 patients), generated significant scientific and media impact.
Ornish published *Dr. Dean Ornish's Program for Reversing Heart Disease* (1990), which became
a bestseller and established his reputation. He subsequently conducted research on prostate
cancer and telomere length, finding similar benefits from comprehensive lifestyle changes.
In 2010, Medicare approved the Ornish programme as a covered cardiac rehabilitation service
— a significant regulatory validation, making it one of the few diet programmes backed by
clinical evidence and government health coverage.
