The **Sonoma Diet** is a Mediterranean-inspired dietary approach developed by registered
dietitian Connie Guttersen, drawing on the food culture of California's Sonoma wine country.
Its philosophy: weight loss through **good food and flavour**, not deprivation.

The practical mechanism: portion control through **plate size** (7-inch breakfast plates,
9-inch lunch/dinner plates), filled primarily with **10 "power foods"** — almonds, bell
peppers, blueberries, broccoli, grapes, olive oil, spinach, strawberries, tomatoes and whole
grains. Wine in moderation is included from Wave 2 onwards.

It is organised in **three waves**: Wave 1 (10 days, strictest, eliminates sugar and alcohol),
Wave 2 (gradual reintroduction, continued weight loss) and Wave 3 (maintenance for life).

## History and origin

Connie Guttersen, a registered dietitian and culinary instructor, developed the Sonoma Diet
drawing on both Mediterranean nutritional research and the food culture of the Sonoma region
in California — a wine and agriculture area with strong culinary traditions. She published
*The Sonoma Diet* in 2005.

The book positioned itself as a more pleasurable alternative to restrictive diets, emphasising
the enjoyment of food as central to sustainable healthy eating — a theme consistent with
Mediterranean dietary philosophy. It achieved considerable commercial success in the United
States and was followed by a cookbook and updated editions.

The Sonoma Diet occupies a niche between Mediterranean diet (broad evidence base, less
structured) and programmes like Weight Watchers (highly structured, commercial). Its emphasis
on flavour, wine country aesthetics and specific power foods differentiate it in the diet
book market, though it has not accumulated the same research base as the Mediterranean or
DASH diets it draws inspiration from.
