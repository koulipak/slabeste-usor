The **Planetary Health Diet** is a dietary framework developed by the **EAT-Lancet
Commission** — a consortium of 37 scientists from 16 countries — and published in 2019.
Its goal is double: to define a diet that is both **optimal for human health** and
**compatible with planetary ecological limits**.

The formula: half the plate in **vegetables and fruit**, a quarter in **whole grains**, a
quarter in **plant proteins** (legumes, nuts, seeds). Very small amounts of animal products
are allowed — approximately 100 g of red meat per week, one egg per day, small portions of
fish and dairy.

It is not a commercial diet programme but a **scientific reference** for nutrition and
food-systems policy. For individuals, it represents a guide towards a predominantly
plant-based diet that is nutritionally complete without requiring complete elimination
of animal products.

## History and origin

The EAT-Lancet Commission was formed in 2017, co-chaired by Harvard professor Walter Willett
and Norwegian researcher Johan Rockström. The commission's mandate was to define, for the
first time with scientific rigour, a global diet that could simultaneously achieve two goals:
protect human health and keep the food system within planetary boundaries (biodiversity,
water, land use, climate).

The result was published in *The Lancet* in January 2019 in a landmark 50-page paper:
"Food in the Anthropocene: the EAT–Lancet Commission on healthy diets from sustainable food
systems." The report proposed specific quantitative targets for each food group based on
evidence from epidemiology, environmental science and food systems modelling.

The response was significant — and divided. Public health and environmental organisations
praised the scientific rigour and ambition. Critics from the meat and dairy industries, as
well as from some low-income countries, challenged the recommendations, arguing that the
model disproportionately affected cultural food traditions and could worsen food insecurity
in regions where animal products are important sources of affordable micronutrients. The
commission acknowledged this tension in follow-up responses. The debate around the report
continues and reflects the broader tension between global dietary transition recommendations
and diverse local realities.
