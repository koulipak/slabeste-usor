**Volumetrics** is a dietary approach developed by nutritional scientist Barbara Rolls at
Pennsylvania State University, based on the concept of **caloric density** — the number of
calories per gram of food. The principle: eat **large volumes** of foods with low caloric
density (soups, vegetables, fruit) and maintain satiety with fewer total calories.

Foods are categorised into four groups by caloric density. Category 1 (below 0.6 kcal/g —
broth soups, most vegetables and fruit) and Category 2 (0.6–1.5 kcal/g — lean protein,
legumes, whole grains) can be eaten freely. Category 3 and 4 (more calorie-dense) require
portion control.

No food is forbidden — only categorised. This approach is one of the **most scientifically
robust** in the diet world: the relationship between food volume, satiety and caloric intake
has been studied extensively in controlled trials, and Volumetrics consistently produces
sustainable weight loss in research settings.

## History and origin

Barbara Rolls is a professor of nutrition at Pennsylvania State University and director of
the Laboratory for the Study of Human Ingestive Behavior, where she has researched satiety,
hunger and portion sizes for over four decades. Her research on caloric density was published
in numerous peer-reviewed articles before she made it accessible to a popular audience.

She published *Volumetrics: Feel Full on Fewer Calories* (2000) and *The Volumetrics Eating
Plan* (2005), which translated her laboratory findings into practical guidelines. The approach
was built explicitly on decades of published research, distinguishing it from most popular
diet books that rely primarily on anecdote or unvalidated theories.

Rolls' work on **portion distortion** — how larger portions lead people to eat more regardless
of hunger — influenced nutrition policy and food labelling discussions in the United States.
Her laboratory's findings that soup and salad at the beginning of a meal reduce total caloric
intake have been replicated in multiple independent studies and are now considered established
nutritional science.

Volumetrics has consistently ranked among the top diets in expert assessments and is
particularly respected in the academic nutrition community for its evidence base, even though
it has not achieved the same popular notoriety as more dramatic or restrictive diets.
