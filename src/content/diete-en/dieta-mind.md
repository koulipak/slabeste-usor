The **MIND diet** (Mediterranean-DASH Intervention for Neurodegenerative Delay) is a dietary
pattern developed specifically to **protect brain health** and reduce the risk of cognitive
decline and Alzheimer's disease. It combines elements of the Mediterranean diet and the DASH
diet, adapted and calibrated for this specific purpose.

The diet defines **10 food groups to eat** (leafy greens, other vegetables, berries, nuts,
olive oil, whole grains, fish, poultry, legumes, wine in moderation) and **5 to limit**
(red meat, butter, cheese, pastries and fast food), with specific quantities and frequencies
for each.

Research published in 2015 suggested significant reductions in Alzheimer's risk for those
who followed it closely. More recent and larger randomised studies confirmed some benefits
but with a more nuanced picture. The MIND diet remains an area of active research; it is not
a rapid weight-loss diet but a sustainable approach to healthy eating.

## History and origin

The MIND diet was developed by Martha Clare Morris, a nutritional epidemiologist at Rush
University Medical Center in Chicago, published in the journal *Alzheimer's & Dementia* in
2015. Morris and her team analysed data from the Rush Memory and Aging Project — a long-term
cohort study of 923 elderly adults — and identified dietary patterns associated with slower
cognitive decline.

Instead of simply recommending Mediterranean or DASH diets, Morris created a hybrid that
retained the shared elements of both but fine-tuned the recommendations for brain health,
giving more weight to foods with specific evidence for cognitive protection: green leafy
vegetables (consistently associated with slower cognitive decline in her previous research)
and berries (rich in flavonoids, which animal and epidemiological studies linked to reduced
neurodegeneration).

The publication attracted significant media and scientific attention. Morris subsequently
led a large randomised controlled trial (the MIND Trial) funded by the National Institutes
of Health, which enrolled over 600 participants and tested whether the MIND diet slows
cognitive decline. The main results, published in 2023 in the *New England Journal of
Medicine*, confirmed that the MIND diet is safe and promotes healthy eating, but did not
show a significant difference from a general healthy diet in slowing cognitive decline in
people without dementia — a more nuanced result than the initial observational findings,
highlighting the complexity of nutritional research in this field.
