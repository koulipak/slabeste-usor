The **cyclical keto diet** (CKD — Cyclic Ketogenic Diet) is a variant of the standard
ketogenic diet designed primarily for **athletes and active people** who practise
high-intensity training. The protocol alternates **5–6 strict keto days** (very few
carbohydrates, the body in ketosis) with **1–2 carb-loading days** (high carbohydrate intake
to replenish muscle glycogen).

The logic: intense exercise (strength training, sprinting, CrossFit) depletes muscle glycogen
reserves. Strict keto does not allow their replenishment. CKD solves this problem by allowing
a controlled carbohydrate window once a week.

For **sedentary people or beginners**, CKD offers no advantage over standard keto and adds
significant complexity. It is a tool for people who already understand the standard ketogenic
diet and have specific performance goals.

## History and origin

The cyclical keto diet emerged from the bodybuilding and competitive sport community in the
late 1980s and 1990s, where practitioners noticed that permanent very-low-carb diets
impaired performance in high-intensity training. Athletes and coaches began experimenting
with carb-loading windows — already used in classic endurance sports — adapted to the keto
context.

The concept was systematised and popularised by Lyle McDonald in his book *The Ketogenic
Diet* (1998), one of the first texts to rigorously analyse the physiological mechanisms
of ketosis and its variants, including cyclical and targeted (TKD) protocols. McDonald drew
on sports science literature and physiology to explain why athletes might benefit from
periodic carbohydrate intake. The book became a reference in the strength sports and
bodybuilding community.

In the 2010s, the explosion of the "keto" trend brought CKD to a wider audience through
forums, YouTube channels and social media. Authors like Dave Asprey (Bulletproof Diet) and
various personal trainers incorporated elements of cyclical keto into their programmes,
adding further variants and interpretations. Today CKD remains popular mainly in the fitness
community, where it coexists with other periodised nutritional strategies.
