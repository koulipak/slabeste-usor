**OMAD** (One Meal A Day) is the most extreme form of intermittent fasting: all daily calories
are consumed in a **single meal** within a **1-hour eating window**, with the remaining
23 hours in a complete fast (water, tea or coffee without calories only).

The claimed appeal: extreme simplicity (only one meal to plan), strong metabolic effects
of the prolonged fast, and significant weight loss. The real risks: it is **very difficult**
to provide all necessary nutrients in a single meal, which creates a long-term deficiency risk;
the prolonged fast can trigger binge-eating episodes in susceptible individuals; and
performance and daily functioning can be significantly affected.

OMAD is not recommended as a long-term lifestyle by major nutritional organisations and
has very limited rigorous clinical data compared to less extreme IF protocols (16:8 or 5:2).

## History and origin

OMAD does not have a specific founding author but emerged organically from the intermittent
fasting community in the 2010s, particularly through online forums and later YouTube channels.
As the 16:8 IF protocol gained popularity, a subset of practitioners began experimenting with
progressively longer fasting windows — 18:6, 20:4, 23:1 — reporting increasingly dramatic
results.

The OMAD term was popularised especially through Reddit (the r/OMAD community formed around
2016 and grew rapidly) and YouTube channels documenting personal transformations. The
community developed its own vocabulary, protocols and culture — including debates about what
can be included in the 1-hour window, whether coffee breaks the fast, and whether OMAD is
compatible with strength training.

There is no single "founding" text for OMAD — its origin is collective and internet-driven,
which differentiates it from commercial diets with authors and books. This also means there
is less structure and accountability: results reported by community members are anecdotal,
and it is left to the individual to navigate the risks. The few academic studies that have
examined very-short-eating-window protocols (22:2 or 23:1) have found short-term metabolic
benefits comparable to standard IF protocols, but raise concerns about the adequacy of
nutritional intake and long-term sustainability.
