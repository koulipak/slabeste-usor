The **Montignac method** is a dietary approach based on **glycaemic index (GI)** — a measure
of how rapidly a food raises blood glucose. Instead of eliminating carbohydrates (as Atkins
or keto do), Montignac proposes *selecting* them: consume low-GI carbohydrates and avoid
high-GI ones, without counting calories.

The mechanism: high-GI foods cause rapid spikes in blood glucose, triggering a large insulin
response, which promotes fat storage and stimulates appetite. Low-GI foods produce gradual
responses, maintaining satiety and keeping insulin levels lower.

The method is organised in **two phases**: Phase 1 (strict, weight loss), where only low-GI
carbohydrates (below 35) are consumed; and Phase 2 (stabilisation and long-term maintenance),
where moderate-GI foods and occasionally higher-GI foods are reintroduced. The method is
notable for accepting **wine in moderation** and **dark chocolate** — unusual elements in
diet books.

## History and origin

Michel Montignac (1944–2010) was a French pharmaceutical executive who, while working in
the industry in the 1980s, documented himself on the role of insulin in obesity and
glycaemic response. He began applying his ideas on himself, losing 16 kg, then shared them
with colleagues and friends. He published his first book, *Comment maigrir en faisant des
repas d'affaires* (How to Lose Weight Eating Business Lunches), in France in 1986 — a title
that targeted an audience he knew well: business professionals who had to eat out.

The book was an immediate success in France and was followed by *Je mange donc je maigris*
(I Eat, Therefore I Lose Weight, 1987), which became an international bestseller translated
into over 40 languages. Montignac opened specialist restaurants in Paris and London,
collaborated with researchers from INSERM and launched a range of food products.

The Montignac method popularised the concept of glycaemic index for the general public years
before scientific literature widely discussed it. The GI concept itself had been introduced
in 1981 by David Jenkins and colleagues at the University of Toronto, but remained confined
to academic literature. Montignac brought it to millions of readers. Critics noted that his
interpretation of the GI's role in obesity was simplified and that the evidence base was
limited; however, subsequent research on GI and metabolic health generally confirmed the
general direction of his thinking, even if the rules were not as simple as he presented them.
