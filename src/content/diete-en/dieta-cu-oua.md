The egg diet is a short-term, high-protein diet in which **eggs are the central food** at
each meal, supplemented with lean meat, fish and low-carb vegetables. Refined carbohydrates,
sugar and starchy foods are completely eliminated.

Its logic: eggs are one of the most protein-rich, satiating and nutritionally complete foods
per unit cost. High protein intake reduces appetite, maintains muscle mass and supports a
caloric deficit without counting calories.

The diet is, however, **very monotonous** and is only suitable as a short-term intervention.
It does not teach sustainable eating habits and presents a significant risk of weight regain
once it ends. There are several popular variants: the "boiled egg" version (only boiled
eggs), the grapefruit egg version, the full low-carb version — none of them has been studied
in rigorous clinical trials.

## History and origin

Unlike systematised dietary protocols backed by academic research, the egg diet belongs to
the category of popular diets circulated through books, magazines and the internet without a
clear founding author. The association between eggs and rapid weight loss grew in the
mid-twentieth century United States, when high-protein diets began to be publicised as
alternatives to calorie restriction.

A popular variant — the "Mayo Clinic Egg Diet" — was attributed without justification to the
Mayo Clinic, which explicitly denied any connection with this regimen. Despite this, the name
persisted in circulation throughout the 1960s–1970s in the United States and spread
internationally through diet books of the era.

The revival of the egg diet in the 2000s and 2010s came with the internet and the growth of
blogging: numerous personal testimonials and short transformation videos generated viral
interest, especially on platforms like YouTube and Pinterest. There are no clinical trials
designed specifically to evaluate the egg diet as such, but its mechanism — high protein
intake leading to caloric deficit through satiety — is well supported by general nutrition
research.
