The dissociated diet (or food-combining diet) is based on the idea that the body digests
**proteins and carbohydrates better when consumed at separate meals**, rather than combined.
Each meal consists exclusively of one food category — carbohydrates OR proteins — plus
freely combinable vegetables.

The **scientific basis** of this principle is disputed: human digestion is designed to process
mixed meals simultaneously and efficiently. Multiple physiological studies have found no
evidence that food combining improves digestion or metabolism. Weight loss on the dissociated
diet is attributable to the caloric restriction that results from the food-combination rules
— not the separation mechanism itself.

In practice, the diet has followers because the rules are **intuitive and rule-based**, without
calorie counting, and because avoiding complex combinations (pizza, sandwiches, pasta with
meat sauce) naturally reduces caloric density.

## History and origin

The theoretical foundation of the dissociated diet was elaborated by American physician
William Howard Hay in 1904. After suffering from a kidney disease he attributed to poor diet,
Hay developed a system he called "proper food combining", based on the idea that proteins
require an acidic medium for digestion while carbohydrates require an alkaline medium, and
that they should therefore not be consumed together.

Hay published his theory in *A New Health Era* (1933), which achieved great popularity in the
United States and Europe. His ideas were not based on controlled research but on personal
observations and the physiological theories of his era, partly outdated. Despite this, the
"Hay Diet" gained significant following in the early twentieth century and spawned a tradition
of food-combining books that continues to this day.

In the 1980s and 1990s, the concept was popularised again through Harvey and Marilyn Diamond's
book *Fit for Life* (1985), which sold millions of copies in the United States and dozens of
countries. In Romania, the dissociated diet became particularly popular in the 1990s through
books published after 1989, when access to Western nutritional literature became possible.
Today it persists mainly as a folk nutrition tradition, despite the lack of scientific support
for its core mechanism.
