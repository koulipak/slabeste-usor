**16/8** intermittent fasting does not tell you *what* to eat, only *when*. You split the day
into two parts: an **8-hour window** during which you have all your meals, and a **16-hour
fasting period** (which includes sleep). For example, you eat between 12:00 and 20:00, then
consume no calories until the following day at noon.

The mechanism by which it aids weight loss is largely straightforward: a shorter window tends
to reduce the total number of calories consumed, without having to count them. It is one of
the most accessible approaches because it bans no foods and costs nothing — but it does
require a little discipline around meal timing.

## History and origin

The specific 16:8 protocol was popularised by Martin Berkhan, a Swedish fitness and nutrition
consultant, through his blog LeanGains.com, which he launched around 2008–2010. Berkhan
promoted the 8-hour eating window in the context of strength training, arguing that extended
overnight fasting combined with training before the first meal optimises body composition —
reducing fat while preserving or increasing muscle mass. His message initially reached the
natural bodybuilding community, then spread far more widely through social media.

Researcher Satchidananda Panda at the Salk Institute for Biological Studies approached
time-restricted eating (TRE) from a different angle: circadian rhythms. Panda and his team
published studies in mice (2012) and in humans (from 2015) showing that aligning the eating
window with the circadian rhythm — eating during the day and fasting at night — had beneficial
metabolic effects independent of caloric restriction. These findings provided a biological
rationale for TRE practices, including 16:8, beyond simple caloric deficit.

Academic and public interest in 16:8 fasting exploded after 2015. Meta-analyses and systematic
reviews published in journals such as *Obesity Reviews* and *Annual Review of Nutrition*
concluded that 16:8 produces similar weight losses to daily caloric restriction, with the
advantage of simplicity — no calorie counting required. Current debates focus on the specific
effects of the timing of the eating window (morning vs. evening) and its interactions with
exercise and sleep.
