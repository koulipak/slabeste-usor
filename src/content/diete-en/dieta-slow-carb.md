The **Slow-Carb Diet** was designed by Tim Ferriss and described in his bestselling book
*The 4-Hour Body* (2010). It is based on **five simple rules**: eat from a short list of
allowed foods (lean protein, legumes, non-starchy vegetables); avoid all white/refined
carbohydrates; avoid fruit; don't drink calories; and take **one mandatory cheat day per
week** where you can eat anything.

The diet's appeal: **simplicity** (a handful of rules, no counting) and **psychological
sustainability** (knowing Saturday is a free day makes Monday–Friday easier to endure).
Legumes are the cornerstone — they are mandatory at most meals as the primary carbohydrate
and protein source.

The evidence base is personal experimentation and community anecdote rather than clinical
trials. However, the underlying mechanisms — low-GI carbohydrates, high protein, no refined
sugar — are consistent with established nutritional research.

## History and origin

Tim Ferriss is an American entrepreneur, author and podcaster best known for the *4-Hour*
book series. He developed the Slow-Carb Diet through years of personal self-experimentation,
tracking his own body composition, blood markers and performance metrics with unusual
rigour for a non-researcher.

*The 4-Hour Body* (2010) presented the Slow-Carb Diet as one component of a broader system
for body optimisation, alongside protocols for rapid fat loss, muscle gain, sleep improvement
and performance enhancement. The book blended personal data, conversations with researchers
and coaches, and anecdotes from others who tested the protocols.

The book reached #1 on the *New York Times* bestseller list and sold millions of copies,
generating a large community of followers who documented their results online. Ferriss built
a substantial blog and podcast presence, further spreading the approach. The Slow-Carb
community on Reddit and various forums accumulated thousands of transformation stories and
practical adaptations of the diet.

Unlike most diet books, Ferriss was transparent about what he didn't know and explicitly
invited readers to run their own experiments and report results — a citizen-science approach
that resonated with a tech-savvy audience and differentiated the Slow-Carb Diet from more
dogmatic diet programmes.
