The **RINA diet** (also known as the **90-day diet**) is a structured programme that cycles
through **four types of days** over 90 days in a fixed repeating pattern:
**protein day → starch day → carbohydrate day → vitamin day**.

The claimed logic: rotating food categories prevents metabolic adaptation and optimises
hormonal response. The scientific basis for this mechanism is disputed — weight loss on
the RINA diet is more likely attributable to the **caloric restriction** produced by the
strict food-category rules.

The programme is popular in Eastern Europe (especially in former Yugoslav countries and Romania)
because it offers a clear, rule-based structure without calorie counting, with a defined
90-day endpoint. Many people find the structure motivating, even if the theoretical underpinning
is not scientifically rigorous.

## History and origin

The 90-day diet was developed by Breda Hrobat and Mojca Poljanšek, two Slovenian nutrition
consultants, who published it in Slovenia in the late 1990s under the name *Dieta 90 dni*.
The name "RINA diet" that became popular in Romania and neighbouring countries refers to
Rina Topič, a Slovenian television personality who promoted and popularised the programme,
becoming its public face. Over time, "RINA diet" became the colloquial name, especially in
the Romanian online space.

The programme spread rapidly through internet forums and word-of-mouth in the 2000s–2010s in
Romania and other Eastern European countries, where it became one of the most searched diets
online. It is notable that its spread was driven almost entirely by digital word-of-mouth,
without major media appearances or clinical studies to support it.

There are no published randomised controlled trials specifically studying the RINA protocol.
The few academic mentions are in observational context, noting its popularity and describing
the protocol without clinical evaluation. Its success in the Romanian online space reflects
a preference for structured, rule-based diets that provide clear guidance without requiring
nutritional knowledge or calorie counting.
