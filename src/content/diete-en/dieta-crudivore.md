The raw food diet (crudivore) means consuming exclusively unprocessed, unboiled, uncooked
foods — or foods prepared at a maximum of 42°C (dehydration, blending). The most common
variant is **vegan**, based on fruit, vegetables, nuts, seeds and sprouts.

Proponents believe that cooking destroys enzymes and some vitamins, reducing the nutritional
value of foods. Scientific evidence on this point is mixed: some nutrients (vitamin C, folate)
are heat-sensitive, but others (lycopene, beta-carotene) become more bioavailable after
cooking.

It is one of the **most restrictive** diets in existence. While it promotes a high consumption
of fruit and vegetables — beneficial in any context — the complete absence of cooked foods
makes it difficult to obtain sufficient protein, calcium, iron and vitamin B12 without
supplementation. It is not recommended for pregnant women, children or those with chronic
diseases without medical advice.

## History and origin

Modern precursors of the raw food movement can be traced to the late nineteenth century.
Maximilian Bircher-Benner, a Swiss physician, promoted the consumption of raw foods around
1900 as preventive and curative therapy at his clinic in Zurich. He is credited with inventing
muesli — a mixture of oat flakes, nuts and raw fruit — and insisted that raw foods contain
"solar energy" lost in cooking, a scientifically unvalidated idea but culturally influential.

The movement gained ground in the United States through Ann Wigmore, who founded the
Hippocrates Health Institute in Boston in 1963 and promoted wheatgrass juice and raw foods as
a solution for various diseases. Gabriel Cousens and David Wolfe continued this line in the
1990s–2000s, writing popular books and building communities around raw vegan philosophy.
These figures contributed to transforming a niche diet into a wellness trend with a global
internet presence.

Today the raw food diet exists in several variants — raw vegan, raw vegetarian, raw omnivore —
and has spawned an industry of dehydrators, high-speed blenders and processed raw products.
Researchers continue to study the effects of consuming raw versus cooked foods, with mixed
results: some nutrients are better preserved raw, others become more bioavailable through
cooking. There are no long-term randomised clinical trials validating the general benefits of
the raw food diet over a conventional balanced diet.
