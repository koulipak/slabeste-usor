The GM diet (General Motors Diet) is a **7-day programme** in which each day is assigned a
specific food category: fruit, vegetables, mixed fruit and vegetables, bananas and milk,
lean meat, and brown rice. The supposed origin is a General Motors employee health programme
from the 1980s — but this claim has never been documented.

The diet promises a loss of **3–7 kg in 7 days**, but the reality is more modest: most of
the rapid weight loss is water and glycogen (which binds water), not fat. After the 7 days,
weight returns quickly when a normal diet is resumed.

The nutritional protocol is **unbalanced**: multiple days provide insufficient protein, fat
and essential micronutrients. There are no peer-reviewed studies validating the mechanisms
or results claimed by the GM diet. We document it here because it is widely searched for
and circulates widely online, not because we recommend it.

## History and origin

The exact origin of the GM diet is unknown. It began circulating in the United States in the
1980s as a physical document (fax or typed memo) attributed to General Motors and the United
States Department of Agriculture — an association that neither organisation has ever
confirmed. The document lacked any author, bibliographic references or scientific
methodology.

The diet spread through photocopying and informal sharing in offices and gyms, then exploded
on the early internet in the 1990s through email chains and forums. The viral mechanism was
simple: a short document, a specific promise (7 days, specific weight loss) and an appeal to
authority (a major corporation and a government agency). These elements are sufficient to
generate viral spread even without any evidence base.

In the 2000s–2010s, the GM diet became particularly popular in South Asia — especially in
India — where it continues to have a large following, documented in surveys and online
searches. Indian nutrition researchers have published several analyses of the diet, generally
critical of its nutritional inadequacy. In Romania, the diet circulated especially through
internet forums in the 2000s–2010s and remains a commonly searched term despite the lack
of scientific support.
