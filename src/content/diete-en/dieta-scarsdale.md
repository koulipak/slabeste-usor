The **Scarsdale diet** is a strict **14-day high-protein, low-calorie programme** developed
by American cardiologist Herman Tarnower of Scarsdale, New York. It provides a **fixed daily
menu** at approximately **1,000 kcal per day** — which is below the minimum nutritional
standards recommended today.

The main appeal: extreme simplicity (no decisions, everything specified) and rapid weight
loss in 14 days. The drawbacks: very restrictive, monotonous, with a low caloric intake
that can cause fatigue and muscle loss, and a significant risk of weight regain after the
programme ends.

The Scarsdale diet is primarily of historical interest today — it was one of the most
popular diets in the late 1970s and early 1980s in the United States and internationally.
Modern nutritional medicine has moved well beyond fixed very-low-calorie menus.

## History and origin

Herman Tarnower was a cardiologist based in Scarsdale, New York, who developed a diet he
gave to his patients and shared informally for years. He co-authored *The Complete Scarsdale
Medical Diet* (1978) with journalist Samm Sinclair Baker. The book became an immediate
bestseller, reaching the top of the *New York Times* chart for 13 consecutive weeks and
selling over 5 million copies in the United States, with translations in multiple languages.

The diet became culturally significant through a tragic incident: in 1980, Herman Tarnower
was shot by Jean Harris, the headmistress of a prestigious private school with whom he had
maintained a long romantic relationship. The murder trial attracted massive public attention
and paradoxically generated further notoriety for the diet bearing the name of the victim.
The case was the subject of books, films and documentaries.

In the early 1980s, the Scarsdale diet was one of the best-known diets in the world. With
the 1990s and the rise of more flexible approaches (Montignac, Mediterranean), its popularity
declined significantly. Today it is mentioned mainly in the context of the history of
diet culture, as an example of the rigid, rule-based approach that dominated popular diet
books before more personalised and sustainable approaches emerged.
