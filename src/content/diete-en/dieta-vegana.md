The **vegan diet** excludes **all animal products** — meat, fish, seafood, dairy, eggs, honey
and any food derived from animals. All nutrition comes from plant sources: vegetables, fruit,
legumes, whole grains, nuts and seeds.

Veganism is both a **diet** and, for many practitioners, a broader **ethical lifestyle**
encompassing clothing, cosmetics and other consumer choices. From a purely nutritional
perspective, a well-planned vegan diet can be healthy at all stages of life — according to
the position papers of major dietetic associations (Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics, British
Dietetic Association). The key word is **well-planned**: without supplementation and careful
nutritional knowledge, significant deficiencies are a real risk.

**Vitamin B12** is the critical supplement — it cannot be obtained reliably from plant foods
alone. Omega-3, vitamin D, iodine, calcium and iron may also require supplementation or
careful food selection.

## History and origin

The word "vegan" was coined by British woodworker Donald Watson in November 1944, when he and
a small group of vegetarians who also avoided dairy founded the Vegan Society in the United
Kingdom. Watson was dissatisfied with the vegetarian movement's acceptance of dairy and eggs
and felt a clear distinction was needed. He combined "veg" from vegetarian with "an" to form
"vegan", explaining that veganism begins with vegetarianism and takes it to its logical
conclusion.

The Vegan Society published its first newsletter, *The Vegan News*, and began the slow
process of building a movement. For decades, veganism was extremely marginal — a 1960s survey
estimated fewer than 100,000 vegans worldwide. Growth was gradual through the 1970s–1990s,
driven by the animal rights movement (particularly Peter Singer's *Animal Liberation*, 1975)
and health consciousness.

The explosion came in the 2010s. The *Cowspiracy* documentary (2014), *What the Health* (2017)
and the Netflix documentary *The Game Changers* (2019) drove huge interest in veganism through
environmental and health arguments. Google Trends shows searches for "vegan" grew tenfold
between 2004 and 2020. In the UK, vegan food product launches increased by over 1,000%
between 2012 and 2019. Today veganism is mainstream in many Western countries, with extensive
food industry investment in plant-based alternatives.
