The DASH diet (**Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension**) was developed specifically to
**lower blood pressure** through diet and is one of the most extensively studied nutritional
protocols. Its principles: a high intake of fruit, vegetables, whole grains and low-fat dairy,
with limited sodium, saturated fat and added sugar.

It is not a rapid weight-loss diet, but its balanced structure naturally leads to a moderate
caloric deficit, supporting gradual and sustainable weight management. It is particularly
suitable for people who also need to manage blood pressure, blood glucose or cholesterol.

## History and origin

The DASH diet emerged from a major research programme funded by the United States National
Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute (NHLBI) in the early 1990s. The research team, led by
Lawrence Appel at Johns Hopkins University, sought to identify a dietary pattern capable of
reducing blood pressure without medication. The programme involved researchers from multiple
major American universities and analysed the effects of various combinations of nutrients,
rather than individual supplements.

The results were published in the prestigious *New England Journal of Medicine* in 1997 in a
landmark article: the diet rich in fruit, vegetables and low-fat dairy, with reduced sodium
and saturated fat, reduced systolic blood pressure by an average of 11.4 mmHg in people with
hypertension — comparable to the effect of some anti-hypertensive drugs. A follow-up study
in 2001 confirmed that reducing sodium on top of the DASH diet produced further benefits.

Since then, the DASH diet has been consistently recommended by the American Heart Association,
the American Diabetes Association and numerous international health guidelines as a first-line
dietary intervention for hypertension, cardiovascular disease risk and type 2 diabetes. It
has consistently ranked among the best diets in annual rankings published by *U.S. News &
World Report*, often in first place or near the top.
